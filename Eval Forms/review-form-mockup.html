Universal Evaluation Domains
Rate each domain using the four-point scale below. Not Measurable (NM) should be used when the application does not provide sufficient evidence to assess a domain — it is not the same as a low score. All four domains are evaluated for every applicant regardless of program.
NM — Not Measurable 1 — Low 2 — Medium 3 — High

Based on the CGS framework adopted across R1 graduate programs: Academic Achievement · Research Experience & Program Fit · Motivation & Mission Alignment · Personal Attributes.

Domain 1 Academic Achievement
Self-reported GPA is shown above for reference. Consider academic trajectory, institutional selectivity, and the rigor of relevant coursework — not the number alone. Account for documented extenuating circumstances where applicable.
Domain 2 Research Experience & Program Fit
Two separate ratings are required. Research experience (track record) and research fit (direction and alignment) are related but distinct — an applicant may have substantial experience but a vague direction, or vice versa.
Consider duration, independence, and range. Publications and presentations are indicators of strength, not requirements for admission.
If research interests are not clearly stated in the application, select Not Measurable rather than Low.
Domain 3 Motivation & Mission Alignment
Consider whether past experience and future goals form a coherent narrative, and whether the applicant demonstrates genuine understanding of what graduate study at this program entails.
Domain 4 Personal Attributes
Two separate ratings are required. Written communication and personal attributes (persistence, initiative, leadership potential) are both evaluated here. Evidence may appear in any part of the application — personal statement, work history, letters, or descriptions of how the applicant overcame obstacles.
Consider the personal statement, writing sample (if provided), and any other written components together.
Consider initiative in research or other pursuits, resilience in the face of obstacles, capacity for independent work, and potential for mentoring or leading others.
All Programs Letters of Recommendation — Overall Quality
The strongest letters are written by someone with direct knowledge of the applicant's academic or research work, include specific examples, and rank the applicant relative to others the writer has supervised. Use Not Measurable only if letters have not been received. Program-specific letter requirements (e.g., individual ratings, faculty origin checks) appear in Part 2.

Soil Science — PhD  Program-Specific Review
The universal domains cover research experience, program fit, motivation, writing, personal attributes, and letter quality. The questions below address the structural and discipline-specific requirements unique to soil science doctoral admissions — particularly faculty advisor availability, which is a prerequisite for all other considerations.
I Faculty Advisor Availability (prerequisite)
A willing faculty advisor is required for acceptance into this program. If no advisor match has been identified, the application should not advance regardless of other scores.
II Scientific & Technical Background
Domain 1 assesses overall academic preparation. This section addresses whether the applicant's specific disciplinary background prepares them for soil science doctoral training.
Relevant disciplines: soil science, chemistry, biology, geology, agronomy, environmental science, microbiology, hydrology, plant science. Minimum typically includes ~12 credits across these areas.
A master's degree (GPA ≥ 3.3) is typically required; an exceptional undergraduate record with advisor support may be considered. Leave at N/A if undergraduate only.
III Lab & Field Skills
Soil science PhDs involve both laboratory analysis and field sampling. Consider any technical skills mentioned in the CV, personal statement, or letters — e.g., soil morphology, lab instrumentation, GIS/remote sensing, statistical modeling, wet chemistry.
At least one letter should come from someone with direct knowledge of the applicant's research or technical work.
IV Funding & Assistantship
Assistantship decisions for applications submitted before January 1 are typically finalized in early February. Note whether funding is sought and whether the prospective advisor has support available.
Final Recommendation — Soil Science PhD
Aim for 2–4 sentences. This may be shared with the committee.
